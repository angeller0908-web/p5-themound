The Mound: Omen of Cthulhu Patch 1.01 Notes – Crossplay Fixes and Performance Boosts

Survival in the eldritch depths of The Mound: Omen of Cthulhu is tough enough without technical hiccups getting in the way. Following the game's initial launch across multiple platforms, the development team at ACE Team has quickly rolled out Patch 1.01 to address some of the most pressing issues raised by the player base.

This first hotfix is currently live across Steam, Xbox, and PlayStation. It focuses heavily on stabilizing the multiplayer experience, improving client performance, and introducing some much-needed quality-of-life adjustments to the pre-expedition preparation phase. Alongside these official fixes, the community has been actively discussing the steep difficulty curve, uncovering the hidden mechanics behind the game's randomized loadouts and map progression. Here is a detailed breakdown of everything changed in Patch 1.01 and how players are adapting to the brutal Lovecraftian world.

Crossplay and Multiplayer Networking Fixes

Cooperative play is the absolute lifeblood of this title. Trying to coordinate a descent into the underground with a squad scattered across different platforms was somewhat rocky during the launch window. Players frequently reported issues with lobbies not appearing or struggling to invite friends from other ecosystems.

Patch 1.01 directly targets these networking friction points. The developers have improved the background matchmaking systems, specifically enhancing the friend finder functionality for crossplay lobbies. More importantly, the update adds a server setting that allows hosts to lock their sessions with a password. If you have been wrestling with the public lobby browser trying to get your console and PC friends into the same instance, this password feature completely bypasses the headache. You can simply create a room, set a pin, and have your squad search for that specific session.

A quick note for PC users: if you were previously playing on the beta branch to test early fixes, the developers advise restarting your Steam client or manually reverting back to the default branch to ensure Patch 1.01 downloads correctly.

Performance Improvements Across Platforms

Handling multiple players, dynamic lighting, and swarms of hallucinatory creatures puts a heavy strain on hardware. The patch notes highlight a series of optimization improvements aimed at keeping frame rates stable when the chaos peaks.

For PC players, the update introduces improved performance profiles, which should help lower-end rigs maintain a playable framerate without sacrificing too much of the eerie atmosphere. Console players on Xbox and PlayStation have also received targeted optimization passes in this hotfix. While the specific technical adjustments were not explicitly detailed, the developers noted that "more are planned in a future update," indicating that console performance will remain an ongoing priority as the community pushes deeper into the endgame content.

Crucial Quality of Life Adjustments

Beyond the backend networking and framerates, Patch 1.01 brings a handful of changes that significantly smooth out the moment-to-moment gameplay loop.

Camera Options for Motion Sickness

First-person horror games often lean heavily on visual effects to build tension. Narrow fields of view, aggressive head bobbing to simulate heavy footsteps, and heavy depth-of-field filters are standard tools for the genre. However, these effects can make some players physically ill.

To combat this, the update introduces a pre-configuration screen on the first launch that lets players choose between a "Cinematic" camera and a "Standard" camera. The standard option strips away some of the heavier, disorienting filters. The developers have specifically stated that this standard camera may help alleviate feelings of delayed interaction or motion sickness. If you have felt a slight input lag when turning your character or interacting with objects, switching to the standard camera might make the controls feel noticeably snappier and more responsive.

Inventory and Economy Changes at the Ox Wagon

Preparation is everything before you step into the darkness. Previously, players had to purchase "Saints" at the woodshop, which added an extra step of running around the hub area before starting a run. Patch 1.01 modifies this flow entirely.

Saints are no longer bought at the woodshop. Instead, they are now equipped directly at the ox wagon right before you initiate an expedition, and their cost has been reduced. This centralizes your pre-run checklist. You can now review your contract, check your gear, and equip your Saints all in one location.

Additionally, PC players have received a specific UI tweak: the ability to organize items in their inventory using a mouse during interactions with the wagon. Managing a grid inventory with keyboard inputs was a point of frustration for many, so native mouse support is a massive improvement for PC loadouts. The patch also ensures that custom key bindings are properly saved between sessions, fixing an annoying bug that forced players to rebind their controls every time they booted up the game.

Addressing the Difficulty Curve: Community Insights

While the developers are busy patching the technical side, the player base is currently slamming headfirst into the game's brutal design choices. Browsing through community forums and Reddit discussions reveals a stark divide between players who love the punishing mechanics and those who feel overwhelmed by the initial difficulty spike.

Dealing with Randomized Loadouts

One of the most debated topics is the game's approach to player equipment. Based on widespread community feedback, The Mound: Omen of Cthulhu does not feature a persistent, fully stocked armory where you can simply pick your favorite assault rifle and stock up on medkits before every drop.

Instead, your starting gear is entirely dependent on the specific contract you choose at the beginning of a run. Contracts dictate your primary objective—such as finding a specific amount of treasure or retrieving a lost logbook from an outpost. Alongside the objective, the contract provides a randomized assortment of weapons, healing items, and ammunition.

This RNG element forces players to adapt constantly. Sometimes the game hands you high-powered firearms and plenty of supplies; other times, you are sent into the dark with barely enough ammo to deal with a single threat. Community veterans suggest leaning into whatever the contract gives you. If you are rolled a weak offensive loadout, you must prioritize evasion over combat. You cannot brute-force your way through an expedition if the contract didn't supply the necessary firepower.

Stealth and Sound Mechanics in the Forest

The difficulty complaints are most vocal regarding the Forest biome. Early player reports describe the enemies in the forest as incredibly durable, absorbing large amounts of damage before going down. More dangerously, the creatures in this area are hyper-sensitive to sound.

The community consensus is clear: firing an unsuppressed weapon in the forest is practically a death sentence. The noise will quickly alert nearby patrols, pulling in more enemies than your randomized ammo pool can handle. Stealth is not just an optional playstyle here; it is an absolute necessity. Players are advising newcomers to keep their guns holstered, communicate quietly, and crouch-walk past threats whenever possible. Engaging the local fauna should only happen when you are cornered and have no other escape route.

Unlocking the Map Roster

Another point of confusion during the launch week has been the progression system. How do you unlock new areas to explore?

According to community guides and early progression trackers, accessing new maps is tied directly to collecting Logbooks during your expeditions. It is currently estimated that there are 13 critical Logbooks scattered throughout the game. Finding these documents and successfully extracting them back to your hub is the primary method for expanding your playable map roster.

This creates a tense risk-reward dynamic during runs. If you find a Logbook deep inside an infested outpost, you have to decide whether your current loadout is strong enough to survive the extraction, or if you should cut your losses and run. Securing all 13 Logbooks safely will require significant coordination with your team and a deep understanding of the game's stealth mechanics.

Final Thoughts on the Current Build

Patch 1.01 is a solid foundational update. By fixing the crossplay networking, adding server passwords, and offering motion sickness camera options, ACE Team has removed a lot of the friction that was keeping players from actually enjoying the horror.

The gameplay itself remains unapologetically difficult. The randomized contract loadouts and the lethal sound mechanics of the enemies ensure that no two runs feel exactly the same. As the community continues to map out the best strategies for survival and hunt down the remaining Logbooks, we expect to see even more refined tactics emerge. Gather your squad, set your server password, and remember to keep quiet when you reach the forest.

FAQ

How do I play with friends across different platforms after the update?

Patch 1.01 improves the crossplay friend finder and adds a server password feature. You can now set a password for your session, making it much easier for your specific group on PC, Xbox, or PlayStation to find your lobby.

What is the difference between the Cinematic and Standard camera?

The Cinematic camera offers a more stylized, heavy-atmosphere view, while the Standard camera removes some visual filters to help reduce feelings of delayed interaction and motion sickness, making it a great accessibility option.

Where do I buy Saints now?

Saints are no longer purchased at the woodshop in town. You now equip them at the ox wagon for a reduced cost right before starting your expedition.
