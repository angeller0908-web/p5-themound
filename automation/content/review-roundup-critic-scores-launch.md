The Mound: Omen of Cthulhu finally arrived on July 15, 2026, and the critical reception is exactly as chaotic as you might expect from a Lovecraftian extraction horror built on Unreal Engine 5. A 69 on Metacritic tells part of the story — but the numbers here need a fair bit of unpacking.

## The Score Spread

Four major outlets published reviews at or near launch, and their takes diverge enough that it genuinely matters which one you read first:

- **CGMagazine — 8/10** — The most enthusiastic review in the batch. The writer's main complaint was early-game difficulty, specifically how tough enemy spawns feel during the opening hours before players find their footing. Beyond that, they praised the "rock-solid foundation" and highlighted that the game avoids the survival-crafting bloat that bogs down many co-op horror titles.

- **DualShockers — 7.5/10** — Described the game as "spectacular" in its use of tricks and cunning, singling out the diversity of ways it can fool players. The review tempers that praise with a real concern: the game can feel frustratingly difficult even in its mid-sections, and the limited meta-progression means knowledge is essentially your only carry-over from run to run. The completion time estimate of five to forty hours (community-reported, not official) reflects just how wide that skill gap can run.

- **IGN — 7/10** — IGN's take is probably the most balanced of the four. They found the [sanity system](sanity-system-explained.html) genuinely clever, praised enemy variety, and called the jungle environment "eerie and majestic." The sticking point was difficulty tuning: the game feels "too oppressive" at times and lacks nuanced options for bringing newer players up to speed in high-level groups.

- **DayOne — 6.5/10** — The lowest score, though the language isn't particularly damning. "Atmospheric setting, great audio and visual design" are acknowledged, but repetitiveness in the gameplay loop eventually becomes a problem. It's the classic extraction-genre tension between procedural replayability and the feeling that you're doing the same thing again.

## What the Scores Actually Mean

A 69 on Metacritic is a polarising place to land. It puts The Mound in roughly the same territory as games that have passionate fanbases but struggle to win over broader audiences — not a disaster, but not a smooth launch either.

The more interesting signal is the *distribution* of scores rather than the average. An 8/10 and a 6.5/10 from equally credible outlets suggest the game is highly dependent on context: who you're playing with, on what hardware, and how much patience you have for punishment in the early hours. That's worth knowing before you spend $29.99.

Speaking of which — there is a 20% launch discount running through July, which brings the game to around $24. That's a meaningful threshold for a title with mixed reviews. [See our pricing and editions breakdown](editions-preorder-guide.html) if you're weighing the value proposition.

## The Xbox Series S Problem

This is specific enough to warrant its own section. Pure Xbox's Craig Reid, who put over 25 hours into the game across platforms, was direct: Xbox Series S owners should skip this one for now, or wait until performance patches arrive. The console "wholly struggles to keep up with the expedition from a visual standpoint" in his assessment.

Xbox Series X does an adequate job after some fiddling with controller settings, but the experience requires a noticeably higher tolerance for configuration than most console players will expect.

No official word on a performance patch timeline has been announced at time of writing (UTC 2026-07-16). This situation is worth watching; ACE Team and NACON have not commented publicly on a fix schedule.

## What Actually Works

Across the critical disagreements, a few things consistently land well:

**The sanity system.** Every player's perception corrupts independently. What you see is not what your teammate sees. That's a genuinely unsettling mechanic that creates real communication friction within squads — the kind of designed tension that co-op horror has rarely pulled off this well. We've covered the specifics of how this plays out in our [sanity system explainer](sanity-system-explained.html).

**Enemy variety.** IGN specifically flagged this as a highlight. The [confirmed creatures](creatures-confirmed-preview.html) include a range of threats that demand different approaches, and the game doesn't lean on a single scary thing repeated endlessly.

**Audio and visual design.** Even the more critical reviews (DayOne's 6.5/10 included) praised the atmosphere. This is a UE5 title and it shows — particularly in the jungle environments that form the primary setting. Whether the hardware can keep up is another question entirely, but the art direction is not in dispute.

**No survival crafting bloat.** CGMagazine's praise for this is worth repeating. The Mound resists the temptation to layer in resource management systems that dilute the horror. You're there to extract and survive, full stop.

## What Needs Work

**Early difficulty curve.** Nearly every review mentions this. The first few hours are rough enough that CGMagazine essentially recommends treating a full three-player squad as compulsory rather than optional. Solo play or two-player runs into the tougher enemy encounters early are described as genuinely punishing.

This connects to the larger question of onboarding. IGN specifically called out the lack of nuanced difficulty options as a problem for groups that include players at different experience levels. If you're planning to bring in friends who are new to extraction horror, expect to do some coaching.

**Limited meta-progression.** DualShockers raised this most explicitly. Knowledge is your primary carry-over between runs. If you're someone who needs to feel mechanically stronger after repeated failure, The Mound may wear on you faster than its extraction-horror peers.

**Repetition in the loop.** DayOne's concern about the gameplay loop becoming repetitive is the hardest to assess without extended hands-on time. Whether this is a design ceiling or a feature of early-access content being limited is not clear yet.

## Who Should Buy It

Right now, the clearest candidate for a day-one purchase is someone with a reliable group of three or four players, decent familiarity with the extraction-horror genre (think [GTFO](vs-gtfo-lethal-company-darktide.html) or Lethal Company), and either a gaming PC or an Xbox Series X. The PC version is reportedly where the game shines most consistently, which aligns with ACE Team's history of prioritising that platform.

If you're on Xbox Series S: wait. That's not speculation — it's a direct recommendation from a reviewer who spent over 25 hours with the game across hardware. Performance patches will likely come; the question is when.

If you're solo or primarily a two-player household: be aware that the early game is designed around larger squads, and the difficulty hasn't been calibrated for smaller groups in a way that reviewers found satisfying.

## Bottom Line

The Mound: Omen of Cthulhu is not a broken game. It's an ambitious one that landed in mixed-review territory because its strengths — sanity mechanics, atmosphere, enemy variety, freedom from crafting bloat — are real, and so are its weaknesses around difficulty accessibility, meta-progression depth, and console performance optimisation. A 69 Metacritic score on launch day for a $29.99 co-op horror title is the kind of number that tends to look better in a few months once patches land and community knowledge builds up.

Whether you wait or jump in now largely depends on your hardware situation and how much runway you're willing to give the early hours.
